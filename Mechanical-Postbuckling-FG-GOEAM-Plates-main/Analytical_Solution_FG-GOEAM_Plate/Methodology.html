Mechanical Postbuckling of Functionally Graded Graphene-Origami–Enabled Auxetic Metamaterial Plates
An Analytical Approach Based on Higher-order Shear Deformation Theory and Quasi-3D Thickness Stretching
📋 Method: Galerkin + Stress Function 📐 Theories: HSDT · Quasi-3D 🔢 Solution: Closed-form postbuckling path

1. Problem Statement

This document describes the complete analytical method used to determine the mechanical postbuckling response of functionally graded graphene-origami–enabled auxetic metamaterial (FG-GOEAM) plates resting on a two-parameter elastic foundation. The plates are subject to uniaxial or biaxial compressive in-plane loading under thermal environments.

Two plate theories are considered in parallel throughout the formulation:

Theory 1 — HSDT (Higher-order Shear Deformation Theory): Adopts the plane-stress hypothesis, i.e., the transverse normal stress and strain are assumed negligible: \(\sigma_z = 0,\ \varepsilon_z = 0\). Stiffness is computed from 2D reduced elastic constants \(Q_{ij}\).
Theory 2 — Quasi-3D (Thickness Stretching): Relaxes the plane-stress assumption and allows \(\varepsilon_z \neq 0\). Full 3D elastic constants \(C_{ij}\) are used. The condition \(\sigma_z = 0\) is subsequently enforced via static condensation, modifying all stiffness matrices to account for Poisson coupling in the thickness direction.

The governing equations are identical in form for both theories; the only difference lies in the stiffness matrices that enter those equations.

2. Plate Geometry and Coordinate System

The plate occupies the domain \(0 \le x \le a\), \(0 \le y \le b\), \(-h/2 \le z \le h/2\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are the in-plane dimensions and \(h\) is the total thickness. The mid-plane lies at \(z = 0\). The plate is discretized numerically into \(N_L = 10\) layers through the thickness for integration purposes. The \(k\)th layer occupies:

\[z_{bot}^{(k)} = \frac{(N_L/2 - k)\,h}{N_L}, \qquad z_{top}^{(k)} = \frac{(N_L/2 - k + 1)\,h}{N_L}, \qquad z_{mid}^{(k)} = \frac{z_{bot}^{(k)} + z_{top}^{(k)}}{2} \tag{1}\]

The shear correction constant used in the HSDT is:

\[c_1 = \frac{4}{3h^2}, \qquad c_2 = 3c_1 = \frac{4}{h^2} \tag{2}\]

These constants arise naturally from the condition that the transverse shear stresses vanish at the top and bottom plate surfaces in the Reddy-type higher-order theory.

The plates are composed of a copper (Cu) matrix reinforced with graphene (Gr) platelets. The material system is designated FG-GOEAM to reflect its functionally graded graphene-origami–enabled auxetic metamaterial character. The constituent material constants used in the model are listed below.

Halpin–Tsai Geometry Parameters

The effective elastic modulus is estimated via the Halpin–Tsai micromechanics model. The geometric parameter \(\xi\) is defined by the platelet aspect ratio, and \(\eta\) is the Halpin–Tsai interaction parameter:

Halpin–Tsai parameters \[\xi = \frac{2\,l_{Gr}}{t_{Gr}}, \qquad \eta = \frac{E_{Gr}/E_{Cu} - 1}{E_{Gr}/E_{Cu} + \xi} \tag{3}\]

Temperature- and Volume-Fraction–Dependent Modification Functions

The effective properties of each layer are not simply rule-of-mixtures quantities; they are modified by three empirical functions that account for coupled temperature and volume-fraction effects specific to the graphene–copper system. Let \(V_{Gr}\) denote the local graphene volume fraction, \(T\) the operating temperature, \(T_0 = 300\,\text{K}\) the reference temperature, and \(H_{Gr}\) the hollow-graphene weight fraction.

Elastic modulus modification function \[f_E = 1.11 - 1.22\,V_{Gr} - 0.134\tfrac{T}{T_0} + 0.559\,V_{Gr}\tfrac{T}{T_0} - 5.5\,H_{Gr}V_{Gr} + 38\,H_{Gr}V_{Gr}^2 - 20.6\,H_{Gr}^2 V_{Gr}^2 \tag{4}\]
Poisson ratio modification function \[f_\nu = 1.01 - 1.43\,V_{Gr} + 0.165\tfrac{T}{T_0} - 16.8\,H_{Gr}V_{Gr} - 1.1\,H_{Gr}V_{Gr}\tfrac{T}{T_0} + 16\,H_{Gr}^2 V_{Gr}^2 \tag{5}\]
Thermal expansion coefficient modification function \[f_\alpha = 0.794 - 16.8\,V_{Gr}^2 - 0.0279\!\left(\tfrac{T}{T_0}\right)^{\!2} + 0.182\,\tfrac{T}{T_0}\!\left(1 + V_{Gr}\right) \tag{6}\]

These three functions are applied pointwise at each layer and each temperature to compute the corrected effective properties below.

Effective Properties of Each Layer

With the volume fraction \(V_{Cu} = 1 - V_{Gr}\) of the copper phase, the effective elastic modulus, Poisson ratio, and thermal expansion coefficient of each layer are:

Effective elastic modulus (Halpin–Tsai + modification) \[E = \frac{\left(1 + \xi\,\eta\,V_{Gr}\right) E_{Cu}\,f_E}{1 - \eta\,V_{Gr}} \tag{7}\]
Effective Poisson ratio and thermal expansion coefficient \[\nu = \left(\nu_{Gr}\,V_{Gr} + \nu_{Cu}\,V_{Cu}\right) f_\nu, \qquad \alpha = \left(\alpha_{Gr}\,V_{Gr} + \alpha_{Cu}\,V_{Cu}\right) f_\alpha \tag{8}\]

Note that \(E\), \(\nu\), and \(\alpha\) are all functions of \(z\) (through \(V_{Gr}(z)\)) and of \(T\). They are evaluated at the mid-plane of each numerical layer.

4. Graphene Distribution Through the Thickness

The local graphene volume fraction \(V_{Gr}(z)\) is not uniform in general. Before specifying the distribution, the global graphene volume fraction \(V_{Gr}^s\) is related to the given graphene weight fraction \(W_{Gr}\) by accounting for the density contrast between graphene and copper:

Weight fraction to volume fraction conversion \[V_{Gr}^s = \frac{W_{Gr}}{W_{Gr} + (\rho_{Gr}/\rho_{Cu})(1 - W_{Gr})} \tag{9}\]

Three distribution patterns are considered. The local volume fraction at the mid-plane \(z_{mid}\) of each layer is assigned as follows:

U-WGr — Uniform distribution \[V_{Gr}(z) = V_{Gr}^s \tag{10a}\]
X-WGr — Graphene concentrated at the surfaces \[V_{Gr}(z) = 2\,V_{Gr}^s \cdot \frac{2\,|z_{mid}|}{h} \tag{10b}\]
O-WGr — Graphene concentrated at the mid-plane \[V_{Gr}(z) = 2\,V_{Gr}^s \left(1 - \frac{2\,|z_{mid}|}{h}\right) \tag{10c}\]

In all three cases the graphene volume fraction integrates to the same global value \(V_{Gr}^s\), ensuring a fair comparison. The U-WGr pattern produces a homogeneous plate (all layers identical), whereas the X-WGr and O-WGr patterns introduce through-thickness property gradients that activate the Quasi-3D correction.

Physical significance: For U-WGr, all material constants are \(z\)-independent. It can be shown analytically that the static condensation in §9 then produces a condensed stiffness exactly equal to the HSDT stiffness — hence U-WGr always yields a ~0% difference between the two theories.

5. Layer-by-Layer Property Evaluation

For each layer \(k = 1, \ldots, N_L\), the following procedure is applied at \(z_{mid}^{(k)}\): (i) evaluate \(V_{Gr}(z_{mid}^{(k)})\) from Eq. (10); (ii) compute the modification functions \(f_E, f_\nu, f_\alpha\) from Eqs. (4)–(6); (iii) evaluate \(E, \nu, \alpha\) from Eqs. (7)–(8). The resulting layer-wise values are then used to construct the constitutive matrices in §7.

6. Plate Displacement Field

A Reddy-type third-order shear deformation theory (HSDT) is employed as the basis for both theories. The displacement field of a material point at position \((x, y, z)\) is:

Displacement field — common to HSDT and Quasi-3D \[u(x,y,z) = u_0 - z\,w_{0,x} + c_1 z^3\!\left(w_{0,x} + \phi_x\right) \tag{11a}\] \[v(x,y,z) = v_0 - z\,w_{0,y} + c_1 z^3\!\left(w_{0,y} + \phi_y\right) \tag{11b}\] \[w(x,y,z) = w_0 \quad \text{(HSDT)}, \qquad \text{or} \qquad w = w_0 + \xi(z)\,\psi \quad \text{(Quasi-3D)} \tag{11c}\]

Here \(u_0\), \(v_0\), \(w_0\) are the mid-plane displacements, \(\phi_x\) and \(\phi_y\) are the rotations of the transverse normals about the \(y\)- and \(x\)-axes, respectively. In HSDT, the transverse displacement \(w\) is independent of \(z\), implying \(\varepsilon_z = 0\). In the Quasi-3D extension, an additional degree of freedom \(\psi\) multiplied by a thickness-stretching shape function \(\xi(z)\) allows \(\varepsilon_z \neq 0\).

The cubic term \(c_1 z^3\) in the in-plane displacements ensures a parabolic variation of the transverse shear strains through the thickness, satisfying traction-free boundary conditions at \(z = \pm h/2\) without a shear correction factor.

7. Constitutive Relations

7.1 HSDT — 2D Plane-Stress Reduced Stiffness

Under the plane-stress assumption \(\sigma_z = 0\), the elastic constants of each layer reduce from the full 3D set to the 2D reduced stiffnesses:

2D reduced stiffness constants (HSDT) \[Q_{11} = Q_{22} = \frac{E}{1 - \nu^2}, \qquad Q_{12} = Q_{21} = \frac{E\,\nu}{1 - \nu^2}, \qquad Q_{66} = Q_{44} = Q_{55} = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)} \tag{12}\]

The denominator \((1-\nu^2)\) reflects the 2D Poisson coupling in the \(x\)–\(y\) plane only. The \(z\)-direction Poisson effect — the tendency of the plate to contract or expand in thickness under in-plane loading — is entirely absent in this formulation.

7.2 Quasi-3D — Full 3D Elasticity Constants Quasi-3D only

When thickness stretching is admitted, the full isotropic 3D elasticity tensor applies. The two independent constants for each layer are:

3D elasticity constants (Quasi-3D) \[C_{11} = C_{22} = C_{33} = \frac{E(1-\nu)}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)}, \qquad C_{12} = C_{13} = C_{23} = \frac{E\,\nu}{(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)} \tag{13}\]

The denominator \((1+\nu)(1-2\nu)\) is strictly larger than \((1-\nu^2)\) for any \(0 < \nu < 0.5\), so \(C_{11} > Q_{11}\) always. However, after static condensation (§9), the net effective stiffness is reduced back toward \(Q_{11}\). For a homogeneous plate the reduction is exact; for a graded plate a residual correction remains, which constitutes the Quasi-3D effect.

7.3 Thermal Stress Resultant Integrands

The thermal increment \(\Delta T = T - T_0\) produces prestress resultants through the plate thickness. For HSDT, the thermal force integrand per unit area of the mid-plane is:

Thermal stress integrand (HSDT) \[\beta(z) = \left(Q_{11}(z) + Q_{12}(z)\right)\alpha(z)\,\Delta T \tag{14}\]

For Quasi-3D, an additional thickness-direction thermal term is needed:

Thickness-direction thermal integrand (Quasi-3D) \[\beta_z(z) = \left(2\,C_{12}(z) + C_{11}(z)\right)\alpha(z)\,\Delta T \tag{15}\]

This term enters the thermal correction to the condensed stress resultants in §9.

8. Plate Stiffness Matrices

The structural stiffness of the plate is characterized by a hierarchy of matrices obtained by integrating the pointwise stiffness constants multiplied by powers of the thickness coordinate \(z\). This integration reflects how different orders of the displacement field couple to the elastic response:

General stiffness integral definition \[\left(A_{ij},\;B_{ij},\;D_{ij},\;E_{ij},\;F_{ij},\;H_{ij}\right) = \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} \bar{Q}_{ij}(z)\,\left(1,\;z,\;z^2,\;z^3,\;z^4,\;z^6\right)dz \tag{16}\]

where \(\bar{Q}_{ij} = Q_{ij}(z)\) for HSDT, or \(\bar{Q}_{ij} = C_{ij}(z)\) for the 3D stiffness matrices (denoted \(A_{ij}^{3D}, \ldots\)) before condensation in the Quasi-3D formulation. The physical roles of each matrix are:

MatrixPower of \(z\)Physical role
$A_{ij}$$z^0$Membrane (extensional) stiffness
$B_{ij}$$z^1$Bending–stretching coupling (zero for symmetric laminates)
$D_{ij}$$z^2$Bending stiffness
$E_{ij}$$z^3$Higher-order bending–stretching coupling
$F_{ij}$$z^4$Higher-order bending stiffness
$H_{ij}$$z^6$Highest-order bending stiffness (from cubic shear term)

Note that the \(z^5\) integral is absent because the HSDT displacement field contains no \(z^5\) term. The \(H_{ij}\) integral uses \(z^6\) because the cubic correction in Eq. (11) introduces a \(z^3\) term whose square is \(z^6\).

Numerically, the integrals are evaluated by summing layer contributions using the midpoint rule over \(N_L = 10\) layers:

Numerical integration — layer summation \[A_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{N_L} Q_{ij}^{(k)}\,\Delta z^{(k)}, \quad B_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{N_L} Q_{ij}^{(k)}\,\frac{(z_{top}^{(k)})^2-(z_{bot}^{(k)})^2}{2}, \quad D_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{N_L} Q_{ij}^{(k)}\,\frac{(z_{top}^{(k)})^3-(z_{bot}^{(k)})^3}{3} \tag{17}\]

and analogously for \(E_{ij}\) (\(z^4/4\)), \(F_{ij}\) (\(z^5/5\)), and \(H_{ij}\) (\(z^7/7\)). Transverse shear stiffness matrices \(A_{44}, D_{44}, F_{44}\) (and their \(55\)-counterparts) are computed using \(Q_{44} = Q_{66}\).

Thermal Force and Moment Resultants

The thermal prestress resultants entering the equilibrium equations are:

Thermal resultants \[N_{xx}^T = N_{yy}^T = \sum_{k=1}^{N_L}\int_{z_{bot}}^{z_{top}}\!\beta(z)\,dz, \quad M_{xx}^T = M_{yy}^T = \sum_{k=1}^{N_L}\int_{z_{bot}}^{z_{top}}\!\beta(z)\,z\,dz, \quad P_{xx}^T = P_{yy}^T = \sum_{k=1}^{N_L}\int_{z_{bot}}^{z_{top}}\!\beta(z)\,z^3\,dz \tag{18}\]

where \(\beta(z)\) is defined in Eq. (14).

9. Static Condensation for Quasi-3D Thickness Stretching Quasi-3D only

The Quasi-3D theory introduces the 3D stiffness matrices \(A_{ij}^{3D}, B_{ij}^{3D}, \ldots, H_{ij}^{3D}\) computed from \(C_{ij}(z)\) via Eq. (17). The condition \(\sigma_z = 0\) must then be imposed a posteriori. From the 3D constitutive law:

\[\sigma_z = C_{12}\,\varepsilon_x + C_{12}\,\varepsilon_y + C_{11}\,\varepsilon_z = 0 \implies \varepsilon_z = -\frac{C_{12}}{C_{11}}\left(\varepsilon_x + \varepsilon_y\right) \tag{19}\]

Substituting Eq. (19) back into the in-plane stress equations yields condensed in-plane stiffnesses. For a plate with through-thickness property variation, the condensation must be carried out at the level of the integrated stiffness matrices (not pointwise), leading to a Schur complement operation.

Step 1 — Inverse operator for the thickness direction

Define the \(2 \times 2\) inverse operator from the through-thickness stiffness block \([A_{11}^{3D}, B_{11}^{3D}; B_{11}^{3D}, D_{11}^{3D}]\):

Condensation inverse operator \[\det K = A_{11}^{3D}\,D_{11}^{3D} - \left(B_{11}^{3D}\right)^2 \tag{20a}\] \[I_{11} = \frac{D_{11}^{3D}}{\det K}, \quad I_{12} = I_{21} = \frac{-B_{11}^{3D}}{\det K}, \quad I_{22} = \frac{A_{11}^{3D}}{\det K} \tag{20b}\]

Step 2 — Condensed stiffness matrices

Each stiffness matrix is reduced by subtracting the coupling contribution through the thickness inverse operator. The general pattern is:

Condensed A matrix \[A_{11}^c = A_{11}^{3D} - A_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{11}\,A_{12}^{3D} + I_{12}\,B_{12}^{3D}\right) - B_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{21}\,A_{12}^{3D} + I_{22}\,B_{12}^{3D}\right) \tag{21a}\] \[A_{12}^c = A_{12}^{3D} - A_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{11}\,A_{12}^{3D} + I_{12}\,B_{12}^{3D}\right) - B_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{21}\,A_{12}^{3D} + I_{22}\,B_{12}^{3D}\right) \tag{21b}\]
Condensed B and D matrices \[B_{11}^c = B_{11}^{3D} - A_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{11}\,B_{12}^{3D} + I_{12}\,D_{12}^{3D}\right) - B_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{21}\,B_{12}^{3D} + I_{22}\,D_{12}^{3D}\right) \tag{21c}\] \[D_{11}^c = D_{11}^{3D} - B_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{11}\,B_{12}^{3D} + I_{12}\,D_{12}^{3D}\right) - D_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{21}\,B_{12}^{3D} + I_{22}\,D_{12}^{3D}\right) \tag{21d}\]
Condensed higher-order matrices E, F, H \[E_{11}^c = E_{11}^{3D} - A_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{11}\,E_{12}^{3D} + I_{12}\,F_{12}^{3D}\right) - B_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{21}\,E_{12}^{3D} + I_{22}\,F_{12}^{3D}\right) \tag{21e}\] \[F_{11}^c = F_{11}^{3D} - B_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{11}\,E_{12}^{3D} + I_{12}\,F_{12}^{3D}\right) - D_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{21}\,E_{12}^{3D} + I_{22}\,F_{12}^{3D}\right) \tag{21f}\] \[H_{11}^c = H_{11}^{3D} - E_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{11}\,E_{12}^{3D} + I_{12}\,F_{12}^{3D}\right) - F_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{21}\,E_{12}^{3D} + I_{22}\,F_{12}^{3D}\right) \tag{21g}\]

All \({}_{22}\), \({}_{12}\), and \({}_{21}\) components follow the same condensation pattern with appropriate substitution of indices. The transverse shear stiffness matrices \(A_{44}, D_{44}, F_{44}\) are not affected by the condensation since they are associated with shear in the \(x\)–\(z\) and \(y\)–\(z\) planes, not the \(z\)-normal direction.

Step 3 — Thermal resultant correction

The in-plane thermal resultants are also corrected by the thickness-direction thermal term \(N_{zz}^T = \int \beta_z\,dz\):

Corrected thermal resultants \[N_{xx}^{T,c} = N_{xx}^T - A_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{11}\,N_{zz}^T\right) - B_{12}^{3D}\!\left(I_{22}\,N_{zz}^T\right) \tag{22}\]

After condensation, the matrices \(A_{ij}^c, B_{ij}^c, D_{ij}^c, E_{ij}^c, F_{ij}^c, H_{ij}^c\) replace their HSDT counterparts in all subsequent equations. From this point onward, the formulation is identical for both theories.

Analytical invariance for U-WGr: For a plate with \(z\)-independent \(C_{ij}\), one has \(B_{11}^{3D} = B_{12}^{3D} = 0\) (symmetric integration), \(A_{11}^{3D} = C_{11}h\), \(D_{11}^{3D} = C_{11}h^3/12\). The condensation then yields \(A_{11}^c = h(C_{11} - C_{12}^2/C_{11})\). Using the identity \(C_{11} - C_{12}^2/C_{11} = E/(1-\nu^2) = Q_{11}\), one recovers \(A_{11}^c = A_{11}^{HSDT}\) exactly. This is why the U-WGr pattern always produces a ~0% difference between the two theories.

10. Governing Equilibrium and Compatibility Equations

The governing equations are derived from the principle of virtual displacements applied to the displacement field of §6 and the constitutive relations of §7. The resulting system couples the transverse deflection \(w_0\) with the in-plane Airy stress function \(f\) through von Kármán-type geometric nonlinearity. The approach decouples the in-plane problem (compatibility equation) from the out-of-plane problem (equilibrium equation).

The compatibility equation (in-plane) and the equilibrium equation (out-of-plane) are expressed in terms of the stress function \(f\) and deflection \(w_0\). Through-thickness integration of the constitutive relations yields the stress resultants and moments, which enter the governing PDEs. After substituting the constitutive and kinematic relations, the equations involve the stiffness matrices \(A_{ij}\), \(B_{ij}\), \(D_{ij}\), \(E_{ij}\), \(F_{ij}\), \(H_{ij}\) computed in §8 (or their condensed versions from §9 for Quasi-3D).

Nonlinear Governing Equations of Motion (Equilibrium)

Using the principle of virtual work, the nonlinear governing equations of motion for the plate under Reddy's HSDT are derived as follows:

In-plane force equilibrium \[\frac{\partial N_{xx}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial N_{xy}}{\partial y} = 0 \tag{27a}\] \[\frac{\partial N_{xy}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial N_{yy}}{\partial y} = 0 \tag{27b}\]
Rotational equilibrium equations \[\frac{\partial M_{xx}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial M_{xy}}{\partial y} - Q_x - c_1 \left(\frac{\partial P_{xx}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial P_{xy}}{\partial y}\right) + 3c_1 R_x = 0 \tag{27c}\] \[\frac{\partial M_{xy}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial M_{yy}}{\partial y} - Q_y - c_1 \left(\frac{\partial P_{xy}}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial P_{yy}}{\partial y}\right) + 3c_1 R_y = 0 \tag{27d}\]
Transverse (out-of-plane) equilibrium equation \[\left(N_{xx} + N_{xx}^T\right)\frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial x^2} + 2N_{xy}\frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial x \partial y} + \left(N_{yy} + N_{yy}^T\right)\frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 M_{xx}}{\partial x^2} + 2\frac{\partial^2 M_{xy}}{\partial x \partial y} + \frac{\partial^2 M_{yy}}{\partial y^2} - c_1\left(\frac{\partial^2 P_{xx}}{\partial x^2} + 2\frac{\partial^2 P_{xy}}{\partial x \partial y} + \frac{\partial^2 P_{yy}}{\partial y^2}\right) + q - q_f = 0 \tag{27e}\]

where \(q\) is the transverse mechanical load (equal to \(0\) in this postbuckling study), and \(q_f\) is the reaction of the two-parameter elastic foundation:

Elastic foundation reaction \[q_f = K_1 w_0 - K_2 \nabla^2 w_0 = K_1 w_0 - K_2 \left(\frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial y^2}\right) \tag{27f}\]

Geometrical Compatibility Equation

To eliminate the in-plane displacements \(u_0\) and \(v_0\) and solve the system in terms of the transverse deflection \(w_0\) and the Airy stress function \(f\), the in-plane equations of motion are satisfied identically by introducing the stress function \(f(x, y)\):

Airy stress function definition \[N_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}, \qquad N_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}, \qquad N_{xy} = -\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} \tag{28}\]

By combining the strain-displacement relationships and mid-plane strain components, the compatibility equation in terms of the strain components is written as:

Strain compatibility relation \[\frac{\partial^2 \varepsilon_{x}^0}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 \varepsilon_{y}^0}{\partial x^2} - \frac{\partial^2 \gamma_{xy}^0}{\partial x \partial y} = \left(\frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial x \partial y}\right)^2 - \frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial x^2}\frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial y^2} \tag{29}\]

Substituting the inverted constitutive relations into Eq. (25) yields the compatibility equation in terms of \(f\), \(w_0\), and the rotation functions (\(\phi_x, \phi_y\)):

Compatibility equation in terms of stress function \[J_{11} \frac{\partial^4 f}{\partial x^4} + J_{12} \frac{\partial^4 f}{\partial x^2 \partial y^2} + J_{13} \frac{\partial^4 f}{\partial y^4} = \left(\frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial x \partial y}\right)^2 - \frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial x^2}\frac{\partial^2 w_0}{\partial y^2} + J_{21} \frac{\partial^4 w_0}{\partial x^4} + J_{22} \frac{\partial^4 w_0}{\partial x^2 \partial y^2} + J_{23} \frac{\partial^4 w_0}{\partial y^4} + J_{31} \frac{\partial^3 \phi_x}{\partial x^3} + J_{32} \left(\frac{\partial^3 \phi_x}{\partial x \partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^3 \phi_y}{\partial x^2 \partial y}\right) + J_{33} \frac{\partial^3 \phi_y}{\partial y^3} \tag{30}\]

Inverted Stiffness Coefficients

The in-plane constitutive equations must be inverted to express the in-plane strains in terms of stress resultants and deflection. This inversion produces a set of coefficients \(C_{ij}\) (not to be confused with the 3D elastic constants of the same name; they are contextually distinct):

Denominator of in-plane stiffness inversion (Eq. 19 in source) \[\mathrm{Denom} = A_{12}\,A_{21} - A_{11}\,A_{22} \tag{27}\]
Inverted stiffness coefficients — rows 1 and 2 \[C_{11} = \frac{A_{12}}{\mathrm{Denom}}, \quad C_{12} = -\frac{A_{22}}{\mathrm{Denom}}, \quad C_{13} = -\frac{A_{12}B_{21} - A_{22}B_{11}}{\mathrm{Denom}}, \quad C_{14} = -\frac{A_{12}B_{22} - A_{22}B_{12}}{\mathrm{Denom}} \tag{28a}\] \[C_{15} = -\frac{A_{12}E_{21} - A_{22}E_{11}}{\mathrm{Denom}}, \quad C_{16} = -\frac{A_{12}E_{22} - A_{22}E_{12}}{\mathrm{Denom}} \tag{28b}\] \[C_{21} = \frac{A_{11}}{-\mathrm{Denom}}, \quad C_{22} = -\frac{A_{21}}{-\mathrm{Denom}}, \quad C_{23} = -\frac{A_{11}B_{21} - A_{21}B_{11}}{-\mathrm{Denom}}, \quad \ldots \tag{28c}\] \[C_{31} = -\frac{1}{A_{66}}, \quad C_{32} = -\frac{B_{66}}{A_{66}}, \quad C_{33} = -\frac{E_{66}}{A_{66}} \tag{28d}\]

11. Stress Function and Geometric Coefficients

Geometric coefficients \(J_{ij}\)

The coefficients \(J_{ij}\) appear in the compatibility equation. They combine the inverted stiffness \(C_{ij}\) with the HSDT constant \(c_1\):

J coefficients (Eq. 21 in source) \[J_{11} = C_{21}, \quad J_{12} = C_{11}+C_{22}-C_{31}, \quad J_{13} = C_{12} \tag{29a}\] \[J_{21} = C_{23}-c_1C_{25}, \quad J_{22} = C_{13}-c_1C_{15}-C_{32}+c_1C_{33}, \quad J_{24} = C_{14}-c_1C_{16} \tag{29b}\] \[J_{31} = -c_1C_{25}, \quad J_{32} = -c_1(C_{15}+C_{26})+2c_1C_{33}, \quad J_{33} = -c_1C_{16} \tag{29c}\]

Stress function solution \(R_{ij}\)

Assuming simply supported boundary conditions and a sinusoidal deflection mode \(w_0 = W\sin(m\pi x/a)\sin(n\pi y/b)\), the Airy stress function is solved analytically. The coefficients of the stress function in terms of the deflection amplitude are (Eq. 28 in source):

Stress function coefficients \[R_{11} = \frac{a^2 n^2}{32\,b^2 J_{11} m^2}, \quad R_{21} = \frac{b^2 m^2}{32\,a^2 J_{13} n^2} \tag{30a}\] \[R_{31} = \frac{-a\,b^4 J_{21}m^3 - a^3 b^2 J_{22}m\,n^2}{\pi(b^4 J_{11}m^4 + a^2 b^2 J_{12}m^2 n^2 + a^4 J_{13}n^4)}, \quad R_{32} = \frac{-a^2 b^3 J_{23}m^2 n - a^4 b\,J_{24}n^3}{\pi(\cdots)} \tag{30b}\] \[R_{33} = \frac{-\pi(b^4 J_{31}m^4 + a^2 b^2 J_{32}m^2 n^2 + a^4 J_{33}n^4)}{\pi(b^4 J_{11}m^4 + a^2 b^2 J_{12}m^2 n^2 + a^4 J_{13}n^4)} \tag{30c}\]

The \(R_{11}\) and \(R_{21}\) coefficients capture the membrane response to the quadratic nonlinear deflection terms. The \(R_{31}\), \(R_{32}\), \(R_{33}\) coefficients govern the response at the fundamental frequency and its interaction with the bending and higher-order modes.

12. Two-Parameter Elastic Foundation

The plate rests on a two-parameter Winkler–Pasternak foundation model. The foundation reaction per unit area is \(q_f = K_1 w_0 - K_2 \nabla^2 w_0\), where \(K_1\) is the Winkler (normal spring) stiffness and \(K_2\) is the Pasternak (shear layer) stiffness. For the three-layer foundation model employed here (upper spring, shear layer, lower spring), the effective parameters are:

Effective foundation parameters \[K_1 = \frac{K_l\,K_u}{K_l + K_u}, \qquad K_2 = \frac{K_s\,K_u}{K_l + K_u} \tag{31}\]

where the dimensional stiffnesses are \(K_u = \bar{K}_u D_m/a^4\), \(K_l = \bar{K}_l D_m/a^4\), \(K_s = \bar{K}_s D_m/a^2\), and \(D_m = E_{Cu}h^3/[12(1-\nu_{Cu}^2)]\) is the copper reference flexural rigidity. The nondimensional parameters \(\bar{K}_u, \bar{K}_l, \bar{K}_s\) are specified as model inputs.

The foundation contribution to the Galerkin integral is (Eq. 32 in source):

Foundation Galerkin integral \(S_2\) \[S_2 = \frac{1}{8\,a\,b\,m\,\pi}\!\left(b^2 K_2 m^2\pi^2 + a^2\!\left(b^2 K_1 + K_2 n^2\pi^2\right)\right)\!\left(-2m\pi + \sin 2m\pi\right) \tag{32}\]

When no foundation is present, \(K_1 = K_2 = 0\) and therefore \(S_2 = 0\).

13. Galerkin Solution Procedure

The governing PDEs are solved via the Galerkin method. The assumed mode shapes for a simply supported rectangular plate satisfy the boundary conditions exactly:

Trial deflection and rotation modes \[w_0 = W\sin\!\tfrac{m\pi x}{a}\sin\!\tfrac{n\pi y}{b}, \quad \phi_x = X\cos\!\tfrac{m\pi x}{a}\sin\!\tfrac{n\pi y}{b}, \quad \phi_y = Y\sin\!\tfrac{m\pi x}{a}\cos\!\tfrac{n\pi y}{b} \tag{33}\]

Substituting into the governing equations and integrating over the plate area, one obtains a nonlinear algebraic system in the amplitudes \(W\), \(X\), \(Y\). The rotational DOFs \(X\) and \(Y\) are eliminated via static condensation of the linear part of the system, leading to the following Galerkin integral coefficients \(V_{ij}\).

Structure of the Galerkin integrals

The Galerkin integrals arise from three coupled equations: the out-of-plane equilibrium (producing \(V_{11}, V_{12}, V_{13}, V_{14}, V_{15}, V_{16}, V_{17}, V_{18}, V_{19}\)), and the two rotational equilibrium equations (producing \(V_{21}, V_{22}, V_{23}, V_{24}\) and \(V_{31}, V_{32}, V_{33}, V_{34}\), respectively). These integrals encode all geometric, material, and foundation effects. Their explicit forms (Eq. 31 in source) are lengthy; representative leading terms are:

Primary buckling Galerkin integral \(V_{11}\) (linear in \(W\)) \[V_{11} = \frac{\pi}{8a^3 b^3 m}\!\left[a^4 n^4\pi^2(R_{33}S_{1,14}+S_{13}) + b^4(m^4\pi^2(S_{11}+R_{33}S_{1,12})-a^2 m^2 S_{14}) + a^2 b^2 n^2(\cdots)\right]\!(2m\pi-\sin 2m\pi) \tag{34}\]
Nonlinear Galerkin integral \(V_{13}\) (cubic in \(W\)) \[V_{13} = -\frac{mn^2\pi^3}{8ab}\!\left[4m\pi(R_{11}+R_{21}) - 2(2R_{11}+R_{21})\sin 2m\pi + R_{11}\sin 4m\pi\right] \tag{35}\]
Load weighting integrals \[V_{18} = -\frac{bm\pi(2m\pi-\sin 2m\pi)}{8a}, \qquad V_{19} = -\frac{an^2\pi(2m\pi-\sin 2m\pi)}{8bm} \tag{36}\]

All \(S_{ij}\) quantities appearing inside \(V_{11}\) and the other integrals are algebraic combinations of the stiffness matrices and the inverted coefficients \(C_{ij}\); their explicit forms span Appendix A of the source and are implemented in the S-coefficient block of the code.

Rotational DOF condensation — \(P_{ij}\) coefficients

Eliminating the rotational amplitudes \(X\) and \(Y\) from the linear part of the Galerkin system gives:

Postbuckling amplitude condensation coefficients (Eq. 34 in source) \[\mathrm{Den}_P = V_{24}\,V_{33} - V_{23}\,V_{34} \tag{37a}\] \[P_{11} = -\frac{V_{24}V_{31} - V_{21}V_{34}}{\mathrm{Den}_P}, \quad P_{12} = -\frac{V_{24}V_{32} - V_{22}V_{34}}{\mathrm{Den}_P} \tag{37b}\] \[P_{21} = -\frac{V_{21}V_{33} - V_{23}V_{31}}{\mathrm{Den}_P}, \quad P_{22} = -\frac{V_{22}V_{33} - V_{23}V_{32}}{\mathrm{Den}_P} \tag{37c}\]

14. Postbuckling Equilibrium Path

After condensation of the rotational DOFs, the load–deflection relation reduces to a polynomial in the normalized deflection amplitude \(W/h\). The three postbuckling coefficients are (Eqs. 38–39 in source):

Common denominator factor \[\mathcal{T} = \frac{1}{\chi_1\,V_{18} + \chi_2\,V_{19}} \tag{38}\]

where \(\chi_1 = -1\) always (axial compression in the \(x\)-direction), and \(\chi_2 = -1\) for biaxial loading or \(\chi_2 = 0\) for uniaxial loading.

Critical (onset) buckling load coefficient \(\lambda_0\) \[\lambda_0 = -\left(S_2 + V_{11} + P_{11}\,V_{14} + P_{21}\,V_{15}\right)\cdot\mathcal{T} \tag{39}\]
Linear postbuckling slope \(\lambda_1\) \[\lambda_1 = -h\left(V_{12} + P_{12}\,V_{14} + P_{22}\,V_{15} + P_{11}\,V_{16} + P_{21}\,V_{17}\right)\cdot\mathcal{T} \tag{40}\]
Nonlinear postbuckling curvature \(\lambda_2\) \[\lambda_2 = -h^2\left(V_{13} + P_{12}\,V_{16} + P_{22}\,V_{17}\right)\cdot\mathcal{T} \tag{41}\]

The complete postbuckling equilibrium path is then:

Postbuckling load–deflection relation (Eq. 38 in source) \[\bar{N} = \lambda_0 + \lambda_1\!\left(\frac{W}{h}\right) + \lambda_2\!\left(\frac{W}{h}\right)^{\!2} \tag{42}\]

This is a quadratic polynomial in the normalized central deflection \(W/h\). For symmetric plates (U-WGr, X-WGr, O-WGr symmetric patterns), the coupling matrices \(B_{ij} = 0\), which forces \(\lambda_1 = 0\) and the path becomes a symmetric parabola opening upward (stable postbuckling). For asymmetric FGM plates \(B_{ij} \neq 0\), the linear term \(\lambda_1\) is in general nonzero.

15. Nondimensionalization

All results are reported in nondimensional form. The critical buckling load is nondimensionalized using the elastic modulus of the reference phase (copper for FG-GOEAM, base metal for power-law FGM) and the plate geometry:

Nondimensional critical buckling load \[\bar{N}_{cr} = \lambda_0\,\frac{a^2}{E_m\,h^3} \tag{43}\]

where \(E_m\) is the reference elastic modulus (\(E_{Cu}\) for FG-GOEAM or \(E_m\) for power-law FGM). This nondimensionalization removes the geometric and stiffness scales, allowing direct comparison across different plate thicknesses, aspect ratios, and material systems.

Summary of Solution Flow

1
Material evaluation Eq. (3)–(8): Halpin–Tsai + modification functions → layer-wise \(E(z), \nu(z), \alpha(z)\)
2
Stiffness integration Eq. (12)–(13), (16)–(18): compute \(Q_{ij}\) or \(C_{ij}\), then integrate → \(A,B,D,E,F,H\) and thermal resultants
3
Static condensation Quasi-3D only Eq. (19)–(22): Schur complement of thickness block → condensed \(A^c, B^c, D^c, E^c, F^c, H^c\)
4
Coefficient computation Eq. (27)–(30): inverted stiffness \(C_{ij}\), geometric \(J_{ij}\), stress function \(R_{ij}\)
5
Galerkin integration Eq. (32)–(37): foundation \(S_2\), all \(V_{ij}\) and rotational condensation \(P_{ij}\)
6
Postbuckling path Eq. (38)–(43): \(\lambda_0, \lambda_1, \lambda_2\) → \(\bar{N}_{cr}\) and full load–deflection curve
Key methodological point: Steps 1–2 and 4–6 are identical for both HSDT and Quasi-3D. Step 3 is the sole addition in the Quasi-3D approach. The magnitude of the difference between the two theories is governed entirely by how much the through-thickness property gradient causes \(\det K\) in Eq. (20) to deviate from the product of diagonal terms — which is zero for a homogeneous plate (U-WGr) and increases with the strength of the gradient (O-WGr > X-WGr > U-WGr).
